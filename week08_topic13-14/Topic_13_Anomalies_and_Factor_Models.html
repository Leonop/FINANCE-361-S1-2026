The CAPM failed empirically. In response, researchers built richer factor models — first the Fama-French 3-factor model, then a proliferating zoo of multi-factor models competing to explain the cross-section of stock returns. This "benchmark wars" arms race was driven by the discovery of hundreds of apparent anomalies: stock characteristics that predict returns even after accounting for standard risk factors. In this module we survey the landscape of factor models, dissect what an anomaly really is, and confront the uncomfortable finding that most anomalies do not survive rigorous replication.

3. Fama-French 3-Factor Model (FF3)

One problem with the CAPM is that it appears to be poor at explaining the actual pattern of realised returns. Recall from Topic 12 that the APT provides a theoretical licence to go searching for empirical factors. Fama and French (1993) took this licence seriously and proposed the 3-factor model:

$$ E[r_i] - r^f = \alpha + \beta_{\text{MKT}} \cdot \text{MKTRF}_t + \beta_{\text{SMB}} \cdot \text{SMB}_t + \beta_{\text{HML}} \cdot \text{HML}_t + \varepsilon_t $$

The three factors are:

  • MKTRF (\(r_m - r_f\)) — The market excess return. This is the single factor from the CAPM. So far it looks just like the CAPM — but wait, there is more.
  • SMB (Small Minus Big) — The return to a portfolio that is long small-capitalisation stocks and short large-capitalisation stocks. This captures the size premium.
  • HML (High Minus Low) — The return to a portfolio that is long high book-to-market stocks and short low book-to-market stocks. This captures the value premium.
Construction detail: SMB and HML are not simple univariate sorts. They involve a two-way sort: stocks are independently sorted by size (2 groups) and by book-to-market (3 groups), creating 6 intersection portfolios. SMB and HML are constructed as weighted averages across these 6 portfolios. See Fama & French (1993) for the precise construction.

The Carhart (1997) model — also known as FFC4 — extends FF3 by adding a momentum factor:

$$ E[r_i] - r^f = \alpha + \beta_{\text{MKT}} \cdot \text{MKTRF}_t + \beta_{\text{SMB}} \cdot \text{SMB}_t + \beta_{\text{HML}} \cdot \text{HML}_t + \beta_{\text{UMD}} \cdot \text{UMD}_t + \varepsilon_t $$

Where UMD (Up Minus Down) is the return to a portfolio long recent winners and short recent losers — the momentum factor.

⚠️ Important caveat: Unlike the CAPM, the Fama-French 3-factor model (and all its successors) is not derived from economic theory. It is a purely empirical description of patterns in average realised returns. The question of whether SMB, HML, and UMD represent compensation for risk or simply mispricing remains deeply contested.

4. The Benchmark Wars

Over time it became clear that FF3 was not the end of the story. Researchers found that FF3 still left many cross-sectional patterns unexplained. This triggered a race to build better benchmark models, leading to what can only be described as the "benchmark wars".

1964 / 1972
CAPM — Capital Asset Pricing Model
Single factor: market excess return (MKTRF). Theoretically derived from mean-variance optimisation. Expected return is linear in market beta.
1993
FF3 — Fama-French 3-Factor Model
Adds SMB (size) and HML (value) to MKTRF. Purely empirical. Motivated by observed patterns in average returns that the CAPM could not explain.
1997
FFC4 — Carhart 4-Factor Model
Extends FF3 with UMD (momentum: Up Minus Down). Still widely used as a benchmark. Momentum has no consensus risk-based interpretation.
2015 (paper 2014)
HXZ4 — Hou, Xue & Zhang q-Factor Model
Replaces HML with two new factors: I/A (Investment) and ROE (Return on Equity / profitability). Motivated by the investment CAPM — a theoretical framework. Drops HML.
2015
FF5 — Fama-French 5-Factor Model
Extends FF3 with CMA (investment) and RMW (profitability). Keeps HML, even though adding it makes little empirical difference. Competes directly with HXZ4.
2018
FF6 — Fama-French 6-Factor Model
Adds UMD (momentum) to FF5. Fama and French themselves acknowledge that UMD has no obvious risk-factor interpretation — yet include it for empirical coverage.
2020
q5 — Hou, Xue & Zhang q5 Model
Extends HXZ4 with a fifth factor for expected profitability growth (\(\Delta\text{ROE}^e\)). Currently the most comprehensive model in the HXZ lineage. Appears to have the edge in explaining anomalies.

HXZ4 vs. FF5: Similar Solutions, Different Philosophy

HXZ4 / q-factor
$$ \alpha + \beta_{\text{MKT}} + \beta_{\text{SMB}} + \beta_{I/A} + \beta_{\text{ROE}} $$
  • Drops HML entirely
  • Motivated by the investment CAPM (theoretical foundation)
  • Appears to have the edge in explaining anomalies
FF5 / 5-factor
$$ \alpha + \beta_{\text{MKT}} + \beta_{\text{SMB}} + \beta_{\text{HML}} + \beta_{\text{CMA}} + \beta_{\text{RMW}} $$
  • Keeps HML "for continuity", despite it adding little
  • Still purely empirical — no theoretical foundation
  • Currently the dominant benchmark in academic finance journals
🔑 Key Philosophical Point: Both FF models and HXZ models are evaluated on their ability to explain average realised returns. But realised returns ≠ expected returns (behavioural finance gives us many reasons to believe they are different). If you want to explain patterns in average realised returns → use FF5 or HXZ. If you want a model of equilibrium required returns → the CAPM or a variant remains the most sensible starting point, despite its empirical failings.

5. Anomalies

What Is an Anomaly?

Back when researchers trusted their models (circa 1970), they would occasionally encounter a variable that predicted stock returns when theory said it shouldn't. They called these deviations "anomalies" — implying the data was wrong and the model must be right.

The terminology has stuck, but the meaning has evolved:

Modern Definition: An anomaly is a sorted portfolio trading strategy that earns positive risk-adjusted returns even after controlling for standard benchmark models (CAPM, FF3, or FF5). More precisely, an anomaly is evidence that some characteristic \(x\) predicts returns in a way that existing benchmark models cannot explain.

The term "anomaly" can refer to three related but distinct things:

  1. A firm-level characteristic — e.g. firm profitability, asset growth, accruals. Used to construct the sorting variable.
  2. A factor loading / regression beta — e.g. beta on market volatility, or on credit spread changes. The sorting variable is constructed by running a time-series regression for each firm.
  3. The resulting trading strategy time-series — the 5M1 portfolio return series itself (e.g. RMW is both an anomaly and a factor in FF5).
💡 "If You Can't Beat Them, Co-opt Them": Most benchmark factors — SMB, HML, RMW, CMA, UMD — started life as anomalies. Researchers incorporated them into benchmark models, transforming "anomalies" into "risk factors". Ideally the new factor has some interpretation as a priced source of systematic risk, but this bar has become increasingly low. UMD (momentum), for instance, has no obvious risk-based interpretation and yet features in multiple benchmark models.

Geertsema & Lu (2020) — The Correlation Structure of Anomalies

A key question is: are these hundreds of anomalies really distinct? Or are many of them measuring the same underlying phenomenon?

Geertsema & Lu (2020) applied hierarchical clustering to the correlation matrix of anomaly strategy returns. Their findings:

  • The monthly returns of anomalies within the same cluster have an average pairwise correlation of 0.57 — they largely move together.
  • The average correlation between cluster strategies is only 0.036 — very different clusters are nearly uncorrelated.
  • The ex ante categorisation used by HXZ (2018) into 6 theory-based categories does not reliably predict which anomalies behave similarly. The within-category correlations in HXZ range from 0.07 to 0.60; the within-cluster correlations in Geertsema & Lu range from 0.41 to 0.89 — the data-driven clusters are much more internally coherent.
Implication: Many of the hundreds of documented anomalies are likely picking up the same few underlying economic forces. Clustering analysis can help reduce the effective dimensionality of the anomaly "zoo".

6. Hou, Xue & Zhang (2018) — "Replicating Anomalies"

"Most anomalies fail to hold up to currently acceptable standards for empirical finance. With microcaps mitigated via NYSE breakpoints and value-weighted returns, 65% of the 452 anomalies in our extensive data library, including 96% of the trading frictions category, cannot clear the single test hurdle of the absolute t-value of 1.96. Imposing the higher multiple test hurdle of 2.78 at the 5% significance level raises the failure rate to 82%... In all, capital markets are more efficient than previously recognized."

— Hou, Xue & Zhang (2018, RFS)

This is a landmark replication study. Replication is a cornerstone of science, yet replication studies are rarely published — journals prefer new results. HXZ (2018) attempted to replicate 452 anomalies from the literature and found that the vast majority could not be confirmed.

🤔 Think First: Look at the replication rates for NYSE-VW (strictest methodology) versus All-EW (most lenient). Why would value-weighted returns with NYSE breakpoints produce much lower replication rates? What types of stocks drive the difference?
Answer: The Microcap Problem

Microcaps are stocks in the bottom 20% of NYSE stocks sorted by market capitalisation each month. They are:

  • Very small and illiquid
  • Very numerous — they make up a disproportionate share of all listed stocks by count
  • Associated with very high equal-weighted returns (partly due to bid-ask bounce, illiquidity premia, and data errors)

When you use all-stock breakpoints + equal weighting, microcaps dominate the extreme portfolios and inflate apparent returns. With NYSE breakpoints + value weighting, microcaps contribute little to portfolio returns and the extreme portfolios contain only genuinely different large stocks. The anomaly signal largely disappears for most strategies once this methodological bias is corrected.

Typical Sample Construction in Contemporary Finance Research

Based on HXZ (2018) and current best practice, a typical empirical finance study uses:

import pandas as pd

# Load raw CRSP monthly data
crsp = pd.read_parquet('crsp_monthly.parquet')

# ── 1. Keep only common domestic US stocks ─────────────────────────────────
# CRSP share codes: 10 = ordinary common share, 11 = ordinary common share
crsp = crsp[crsp['shrcd'].isin([10, 11])]

# ── 2. Listed on NYSE, AMEX, or NASDAQ ─────────────────────────────────────
# CRSP exchange codes: 1 = NYSE, 2 = AMEX, 3 = NASDAQ
crsp = crsp[crsp['exchcd'].isin([1, 2, 3])]

# ── 3. Require non-missing returns and market cap ───────────────────────────
crsp = crsp.dropna(subset=['ret', 'mktcap'])

# ── 4. Compute NYSE size breakpoints (20th percentile = microcap threshold) ─
nyse = crsp[crsp['exchcd'] == 1].copy()
nyse_p20 = nyse.groupby('date')['mktcap'].quantile(0.20).rename('nyse_p20')
crsp = crsp.merge(nyse_p20, on='date')

# ── 5. Flag microcaps ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
crsp['is_microcap'] = crsp['mktcap'] < crsp['nyse_p20']

# For value-weighted results, drop microcaps explicitly:
sample_vw = crsp[~crsp['is_microcap']].copy()
# For equal-weighted results with NYSE breakpoints, keep all but
# use NYSE-only stocks to calculate quantile breakpoints.

# ── Optional: Drop finance and utilities ────────────────────────────────────
# CRSP / Compustat SIC codes:
# Finance firms: 6000-6999, Utilities: 4900-4999
crsp = crsp[~crsp['sic'].between(6000, 6999)]  # optional
crsp = crsp[~crsp['sic'].between(4900, 4999)]  # optional

print(f"Final sample: {crsp.shape[0]:,} stock-month observations")
# In practice, less than half of all listed stocks survive these filters.
Takeaway: A rigorous anomaly study should:
  1. Use NYSE breakpoints to avoid microcap contamination
  2. Report value-weighted returns (equal-weighted as a robustness check)
  3. Apply the multiple-testing correction |t| ≥ 2.78 rather than the naive 1.96 threshold
  4. Verify results over the extended sample, not just the original study period

7. Final Thoughts

Trying to understand the factors driving average returns is an ongoing project. The main difficulty is untangling three confounding forces:

✅ Risk Compensation

Compensation for bearing systematic risk. Should be in benchmark pricing models. E.g., the size premium reflects the higher distress risk of small firms.

⚠️ Predictable Mispricing

Persistent behavioural biases or institutional constraints that prevent prices from reflecting true fundamental value. Should not be in benchmark models — it represents genuine alpha.

❌ Data Mining

"Results" obtained by fitting models to noise through brute force — running thousands of regressions until something looks significant. Should not be in any model. HXZ (2018) suggest this is responsible for a large fraction of published anomalies.

Looking Forward — Machine Learning: In terms of return predictability, research interest has increasingly moved toward more powerful approaches from machine learning. Methods like random forests, gradient boosting, and deep neural networks can simultaneously consider hundreds of characteristics and their interactions in ways that traditional sorted portfolio methodology cannot. This will be the subject of a future topic.

Summary: Where Does This Leave Us?

  • The CAPM is theoretically elegant but empirically weak.
  • Multi-factor models (FF3, FF5, HXZ4) do better at explaining cross-sectional variation in realised returns — but are purely empirical, not theoretical.
  • Hundreds of "anomalies" have been reported; most do not survive rigorous replication.
  • The benchmark wars continue: HXZ models appear to have the edge for explaining anomalies; the CAPM remains the starting point for estimating required returns in corporate finance and valuation.
  • Rigorous methodology — NYSE breakpoints, value-weighted returns, multiple-testing correction — is essential to separate genuine signal from noise.
