3. What Is Machine Learning?

Three terms are often used interchangeably but are not the same:

🤖 Artificial Intelligence (AI)
The science of creating systems that exhibit aspects of intelligence — reasoning, perception, language, problem-solving.
⚙️ Machine Learning (ML)
A branch of AI. Models that learn directly from data without being explicitly programmed with rules.
🧠 Deep Learning (DL)
A branch of ML using multi-layer neural networks. Powers modern image recognition, LLMs, etc.

AI ⊃ ML ⊃ Deep Learning. Each inner ring is a specialisation of the outer one.

Is AI "intelligent"? The question of whether machines truly understand has been debated for decades. A practical answer: DALL-E (OpenAI, 2021) could produce a convincing image of "a baby daikon radish in a tutu walking a dog" — an object combination that has never appeared in a photograph. Producing that image requires something that functions like understanding of composition, anatomy, and style. Whether or not it is "true" understanding is a philosophical debate; the practical capability is not in doubt.

Key distinction: model specification vs. data-driven learning

A traditional model like OLS requires the researcher to specify the model: which variables to include, what functional form (linear, log, quadratic), which interactions. The model is then estimated given that fixed structure.

An ML model allows the data to determine the functional form. A decision tree, for instance, will discover its own non-linear breakpoints. A neural network discovers its own feature representations through multiple layers of transformation. The researcher still makes design choices (architecture, regularisation), but the model has far greater freedom to fit patterns in the data.

The cost: this flexibility makes ML models much more prone to overfitting — fitting the noise in training data rather than the true signal.

4. Machine Learning and Return Predictability

Applying ML to predict returns is not new — books on the topic were published in the 1990s. What is new is the sudden success of ML in domains like computer vision and NLP, which has led people to ask: "Why don't we use this to predict returns — and become rich?"

🤔 Think First: Before reading the 9 challenges below, list 3 reasons why predicting stock returns with ML might be harder than predicting, say, tomorrow's weather. Write your answers before continuing.

There are at least 9 structural reasons why finance is a uniquely difficult domain for ML:

1
Extreme Noise
The signal-to-noise ratio in asset returns is approximately 0.03 — far lower than almost any other ML application domain. There is almost no signal to find.
2
Reflexivity
When you trade on a model, you directly change the data-generating process (DGP). The market adapts, making historical patterns increasingly useless. Soros calls this reflexivity; statisticians call it non-stationarity.
3
Zero-Sum Game
Short-term trading is a zero-sum game. Every dollar you profit is a dollar someone else loses. This creates a never-ending arms race — unless you are Renaissance Capital.
4
Black Swans
Financial returns exhibit fat tails — unusually extreme events are unusually common. Most ML models assume well-behaved distributions; tail risk breaks them catastrophically.
5
Finite Data + Overfitting
Financial data arrives at a finite rate (one month per month). You cannot run randomised experiments. Data mining and overfitting cannot be controlled — see also Challenge 2.
6
No Experiments Allowed
Regulators interpret deliberate market "experiments" as manipulation. You cannot run A/B tests on trading strategies in the way you can run clinical trials for a drug.
7
Time Decay of Strategies
By the time you have enough data to confirm a strategy is significantly profitable, it has almost certainly already been discovered and arbitraged away by larger, faster players.
8
Commoditized Tools
The best ML frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn) are open-source and free. Market data is available from standard providers. What, exactly, is your competitive edge?
9
Talent Arms Race
Average ML skills are useless in a zero-sum game. Any excess returns will be spent on retaining the best ML talent money can buy — a cost that erodes the advantage.
⚠️ But here's the catch: Despite all of the above, workers who can use AI will replace workers who cannot. The goal of this module is not to train you to beat Renaissance Capital — it is to ensure you can understand, critique, and apply ML methods when they are relevant. That is a career-defining skill in 2025 and beyond.

5. Actually Doing ML

In principle, ML can be implemented in any Turing-complete language. In practice, the most powerful ML frameworks — PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn, XGBoost — require Python. Python is the lingua franca of ML.

A note on Python: Python was named after the Monty Python BBC comedy programme (not the snake). It was created by Dutch mathematician Guido van Rossum in the late 1980s. It is often described as "easy to learn", which is true for simple programs — but the full language is one of the most sophisticated programming languages in existence, supporting procedural, object-oriented, and functional paradigms.

The ML Pipeline

Every ML project follows a common structure. Understanding this pipeline is more important than knowing any specific algorithm:

1. Load Data
→
2. Engineer Features
→
3. Train / Test Split
→
4. Fit Model
→
5. Predict on Test
→
6. Evaluate (MSE / R²)

The critical rule: the model never sees test data during training. If test data "leaks" into training (e.g. through normalisation using the full dataset), the evaluation is invalid. In finance, this is analogous to the look-ahead bias problem from the sorted portfolio methodology.

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The central challenge in supervised ML is the bias-variance tradeoff:

$$ \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{y} - y)^2\right]}_{\text{Expected test error}} = \underbrace{\text{Bias}^2}_{\text{underfitting}} + \underbrace{\text{Variance}}_{\text{overfitting}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2_\varepsilon}_{\text{irreducible}} $$
  • Bias: Error from overly simplistic assumptions. A linear model fit to genuinely non-linear data has high bias. Underfitting.
  • Variance: Error from excessive sensitivity to small fluctuations in training data. A deeply grown decision tree memorises training noise. Overfitting.
  • Irreducible error (\(\sigma^2_\varepsilon\)): Noise inherent in the data that no model can eliminate. In finance, this is the dominant component.

Bias-variance tradeoff. Training error (blue) decreases monotonically with model complexity. Test error (red) follows a U-shape — the optimal model sits in the valley. Irreducible noise is the grey dashed floor.

📌 Finance context: Because the signal-to-noise ratio in finance is ~0.03, the "optimal complexity" point is far to the left of the chart — very simple models. Highly complex deep neural networks will overfit financial data catastrophically unless they are heavily regularised (e.g., dropout, early stopping, L2 penalties).

A Complete 27-Line Python ML Example

The following program illustrates the entire ML pipeline — downloading real data, training five different models, and comparing their out-of-sample accuracy — in under 30 lines of Python.

Task: Predict next month's market excess return (\(R^{\text{MKT}}_{t+1}\)) using the current month's HXZ q5 factors (\(R^{\text{MKT}}_t, R^{\text{ME}}_t, R^{\text{IA}}_t, R^{\text{ROE}}_t, R^{\text{EG}}_t\)) plus the risk-free rate (\(R^F_t\)).

🤔 Think First: Five models are about to compete: OLS, Ridge regression, a Decision Tree, Gradient Boosting, and a Neural Network. Rank them 1–5 from lowest to highest expected MSE (best to worst). Write your prediction before scrolling to the results. 
import pandas as pd
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression, Ridge
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeRegressor
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingRegressor
from sklearn.neural_network import MLPRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error

# ── 1. Download monthly q5 factor data from global-q.org ────────────────────
url = ("http://global-q.org/uploads/1/2/2/6/122679606/"
       "q5_factors_monthly_2019a.csv")
df = pd.read_csv(url)
print("Columns:", list(df.columns))

# ── 2. Target: next-month market return ──────────────────────────────────────
df["R_FWD_MKT"] = df["R_MKT"].shift(-1)

# ── 3. Train / Test split (pre-2000 = train; 2000+ = test) ──────────────────
D_train = df[df["year"] < 2000].dropna()
D_test  = df[df["year"] >= 2000].dropna()

# ── 4. Separate features (X) from target (y) ────────────────────────────────
drop_cols = ["R_FWD_MKT", "year", "month"]
y_train, y_test = D_train["R_FWD_MKT"], D_test["R_FWD_MKT"]
X_train = D_train.drop(columns=drop_cols)
X_test  = D_test.drop(columns=drop_cols)

# ── 5. Define five models ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
models = {
    "OLS":       LinearRegression(),
    "Ridge":     Ridge(alpha=2),
    "Tree":      DecisionTreeRegressor(random_state=0),
    "GBM":       GradientBoostingRegressor(random_state=0, n_estimators=10),
    "NeuralNet": MLPRegressor(random_state=0, early_stopping=True),
}

# ── 6. Train → predict → report MSE ─────────────────────────────────────────
for name, model in models.items():
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    preds = model.predict(X_test)
    mse   = mean_squared_error(y_test, preds)
    print(f"{name:12s}  MSE = {mse:.2f}")

Model Output

Running the code above produces the following results (your figures may differ slightly depending on package versions):

Model Type MSE (out-of-sample) Rank
GBM Ensemble (gradient boosted trees) 18.41 🥇 Best
Ridge Linear (regularised OLS) 19.39 2nd
OLS Linear (ordinary least squares) 19.45 3rd
NeuralNet Multilayer perceptron 19.82 4th
Tree Single decision tree 37.14 ❌ Worst (overfit)

MSE = Mean Squared Error on out-of-sample test data (year ≥ 2000). Lower is better. Note: sklearn's mean_squared_error returns MSE by default, not RMSE.

Out-of-sample MSE for five models. GBM achieves the lowest MSE; the unregularised single Decision Tree overfits severely.

Interpretation:
  • GBM wins — gradient boosting can capture mild non-linearities while controlling overfitting through the ensemble mechanism.
  • Decision Tree loses badly — a single deep tree memorises the training set; its test MSE is nearly double everyone else's. This is a textbook example of overfitting.
  • Ridge slightly beats OLS — even mild regularisation (penalising large coefficients) helps slightly, consistent with the high-noise environment.
  • All models are bad in absolute terms — the MSEs are enormous. This is a toy example with very limited features and only ~600 observations.

What We Did Not Do Properly

⚠️ This is a toy example. A real ML study would also:
  • Tune hyperparameters on a separate validation set (not the test set). E.g., the alpha in Ridge and n_estimators in GBM should be chosen by cross-validation.
  • Standardise inputs before feeding them to the neural network (mean = 0, std = 1). We skipped this.
  • Use many more features — GKX (2020) use 94 stock-level characteristics. We used just 5 factor returns.
  • Use many more observations — ML works best with thousands or millions of observations. We have ~600 monthly data points.

Your Question

"Why does gradient boosting tend to outperform OLS for predicting stock returns, given that GBM is a much more complex model and complex models tend to overfit?"

🤖 AI-Generated Answer (Claude)

Gradient boosting outperforms OLS in this setting because it can capture mild non-linearities and interaction effects that OLS cannot. Unlike a single deep decision tree (which overfits severely), GBM builds many shallow trees sequentially, each correcting the residual errors of the last. The ensemble structure limits variance — shallow trees have low variance individually; the boosting corrects bias. Ridge regression beats plain OLS for the same reason: regularisation penalises extreme coefficients, keeping variance low in a high-noise environment. GBM essentially builds in a form of regularisation through the combination of shallow trees and learning rate shrinkage.

🔍 AI Critique Exercise

A student asked an AI: "My decision tree has training R² = 0.91 and test R² = 0.02. What is wrong?" The AI responded:

"Decision trees perform poorly on financial data because they use non-parametric methods. Non-parametric methods assume nothing about the distribution of the data, which makes them less effective for time-series problems where past patterns tend to repeat."

Identify what is wrong with this answer. What is the actual explanation for the gap between training and test R², and what would you do to fix it?

Reveal correct answer

The AI's explanation is wrong. The problem is overfitting, not "non-parametric methods being unsuitable for time series". The symptoms are classic: near-perfect training performance (R² = 0.91) but near-zero test performance (R² = 0.02), which means the tree has memorised the training noise rather than learned a generalisable pattern.

Random forests and gradient boosted trees are also non-parametric and ensemble methods built from decision trees — and they work quite well on financial data. The issue is not the non-parametric nature, but that an unconstrained single tree grows until every training observation is perfectly classified, leaving no room for generalisation.

Fixes: (1) Constrain max_depth (e.g., to 3–5). (2) Use cross-validation to select the depth. (3) Use an ensemble method like random forests or GBM instead of a single tree.

6. Gu, Kelly & Xiu (2020) — "Empirical Asset Pricing via Machine Learning"

"We perform a comparative analysis of machine learning methods for the canonical problem of empirical asset pricing: measuring asset risk premiums. We demonstrate large economic gains to investors using machine learning forecasts, in some cases doubling the performance of leading regression-based strategies from the literature. We identify the best-performing methods (trees and neural networks) and trace their predictive gains to allowing non-linear predictor interactions missed by other methods. All methods agree on the same set of dominant predictive signals, a set that includes variations on momentum, liquidity, and volatility."

— Gu, Kelly & Xiu (2020, Review of Financial Studies)

Why This Paper Matters

Predicting financial returns with ML is not new — books on "neural networks in the capital markets" appeared in the 1990s. The problem with earlier work was that it lacked economic credibility: simply forecasting returns was not sufficient for publication in top finance journals. GKX solved this by:

  • Framing the question in the language of finance: "Measuring asset risk premiums" rather than "predicting future returns." A subtle but important distinction.
  • Using proper ML methodology: Rigorous train/validation/test splits, hyperparameter tuning on a hold-out validation set, and standardised inputs.
  • Providing economic interpretation: Measuring which characteristics matter most via variable importance scores.
  • Covering a range of methods: OLS, Ridge, LASSO, Elastic Net, PCR, PLS, GBRT, Random Forests, Neural Networks — a genuine horse race.

Key Findings

🤔 Think First: From the abstract alone, predict the paper's top 3 findings. Write them down before reading the list below.
✅ ML beats OLS

ML methods outperform traditional regression-based strategies for return prediction out-of-sample. Gains are economically large — sometimes doubling Sharpe ratios.

✅ Non-linearity matters

The predictive advantage of ML over OLS comes primarily from capturing non-linear predictor interactions that linear models miss by design.

✅ Best methods: Trees & NNs

Gradient boosted regression trees (GBRT) and neural networks consistently outperform linear methods. Ridge and LASSO are better than plain OLS but fall short of the best ML methods.

✅ Dominant signals: MOM, LIQ, VOL

All methods agree: the most important predictors involve variations on momentum, liquidity, and volatility. This is consistent with anomaly literature (Topic 13).

📚 Beyond the Textbook — Where This Research Is Heading

Seminal Papers

  • Gu, Kelly & Xiu (2021), "Autoencoder Asset Pricing Models", Journal of Econometrics. Extends GKX to learn latent risk factors directly from data using deep autoencoders — replacing the hand-crafted FF5/HXZ factors with data-learned representations. [Link]
  • Kozak, Nagel & Santosh (2020), "Shrinking the Cross-Section", Journal of Financial Economics. Shows that the dozens of factors in the zoo can be compressed to a few principal components using Bayesian regularisation — bridging ML and structural asset pricing. [Link]
  • Chen, Pelger & Zhu (2024), "Deep Learning in Asset Pricing", Management Science. Uses deep neural networks to learn the stochastic discount factor directly — a more theoretically grounded approach than the GKX prediction framework.

Industry Practice

  • Two Sigma — quantitative hedge fund that pioneered systematic ML-based trading. Their blog posts and white papers are unusually informative for a hedge fund: twosigma.com/insights.
  • Renaissance Technologies (Medallion Fund) — the best-performing investment fund in history, with annualised returns above 60% before fees for 30+ years. Widely believed to use proprietary ML and signal processing techniques. Very little is known publicly.
  • Man AHL, WorldQuant, Acadian — other major quantitative managers with published research on factor models and systematic strategies.
